# Tomato Agronomy & Irrigation Guidelines

## Introduction
Tomatoes (*Solanum lycopersicum*) are highly sensitive to water availability. Proper scheduling prevents diseases like blossom end rot, crop cracking, and root rot.

## Soil and Moisture Requirements
- **Preferred Soil Type**: Loamy, well-draining soil with a pH of 6.0 to 6.8. Sandy loam is also acceptable but requires more frequent, lighter irrigations.
- **Root Zone Depth**: Active root zone is in the top 30 to 45 cm, although roots can reach up to 60 cm.
- **Moisture Depletion Limit**: Irrigate when soil moisture depletion in the root zone reaches 40-50% of the available water capacity.

## Growth Stage Irrigation Management

### 1. Establishment / Transplant Stage (Days 1–14)
- **Water Need**: Low volume but high frequency.
- **Strategy**: Keep the top soil layer (0-15 cm) moist to help roots establish. Irrigate daily or every other day with 2–4 mm.
- **Risk**: Overwatering leads to damping-off; underwatering leads to transplant shock.

### 2. Vegetative Stage (Days 15–45)
- **Water Need**: Moderate (3–5 mm/day).
- **Strategy**: Encourage deep rooting by letting the top few centimeters of soil dry out slightly before watering. Less frequent but deeper watering (every 2-3 days).
- **Risk**: Continuous light watering results in shallow roots.

### 3. Flowering and Fruit Set Stage (Days 46–70)
- **Water Need**: High and critical (5–7 mm/day).
- **Strategy**: Keep soil moisture uniform. Fluctuations in moisture level during flowering lead to blossom drop and blossom-end rot (calcium deficiency caused by poor water transport).
- **Risk**: Avoid water stress. Any dry-out period followed by heavy watering causes fruit splitting/cracking.

### 4. Yield Formation & Ripening (Days 71+)
- **Water Need**: High to declining (4–6 mm/day, decreasing near harvest).
- **Strategy**: Maintain adequate moisture during fruit expansion. Reduce watering frequency as fruit ripens to enhance flavor/sugar content and prevent cracking.
- **Risk**: Excessive watering at late stages dilutes flavor and promotes cracking or rot.
